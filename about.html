I founded Damaros from inside clinical medicine.

Clinical trial execution is not a generic automation problem. It is a regulated clinical workflow problem where protocol logic, patient evidence, human judgment, privacy boundaries, and auditability determine whether AI can be trusted at all.

Discovery is accelerating. Protocols are becoming more complex. Patient evidence is expanding across fragmented systems. Sites are being asked to operationalize work their software stack was never built to run.

That gap becomes infrastructure.

I am building Damaros from the intersection of medicine, AI systems, and institutional trust. From inside the clinical environment, the missing layer is visible earlier: not another dashboard, not another chatbot, not another workflow wrapper, but a governed execution system for the moment protocol intent meets patient evidence.

The next clinical AI companies will not be built by wrapping models around workflows they do not understand. Regulated medicine does not adopt intelligence because it sounds powerful. It adopts systems that understand the work deeply enough to encode boundaries, provenance, human review, and replay.

Clinical trials expose the failure first. They compress the hospital's execution problem into one chain: therapeutic intent, fragmented evidence, site review, institutional risk, and decisions reconstructed after the fact.

Damaros begins there because the need is immediate, the cost is visible, and the workflow is already breaking.

The doctrine stays constant: the model is not the system of record.

Damaros is building the AI-native execution infrastructure for clinical research: protocol logic, patient evidence, deterministic screening, human review, and Replay in one governed chain.
